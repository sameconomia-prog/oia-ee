Future of Jobs in Latin American Higher Education: A Synthesis Report
World Economic Forum / IDB joint working paper, March 2026

[Executive Summary]

This report synthesizes findings from 47 employer surveys (n=12,840 firms) and 31 academic-program audits across 8 Latin American economies. We focus on the displacement and emergence of skills attributable to generative AI deployment between 2023 and 2026, and project trajectories to 2030.

Key headline findings:

1. Across our sample, 47% of accounting and bookkeeping tasks are now performed by AI-assisted software in firms with more than 50 employees, up from 12% in 2023 (p.11, Table 2.1).

2. The share of legal junior tasks (contract drafting, jurisprudence search, due diligence) automated by GenAI has reached 38% in regional law firms with international clients (p.14).

3. Demand for "AI auditors" — professionals who can validate machine-generated outputs in regulated sectors — grew 312% in the same period (p.18, Figure 3.4). Most postings require a background in either accounting, law, or statistics, but no current Latin American university program offers a dedicated track.

4. Among medical doctors, the deployment of clinical decision-support tools is now standard in 28% of Mexican private hospitals; this has not reduced employment but has shifted skill requirements toward differential diagnosis with AI assistance (p.22).

5. The half-life of a software-engineering curriculum, defined as the median time before a graduate's technical stack becomes industry-obsolete, has shortened from 4.2 years in 2018 to 2.1 years in 2026 (p.31).

[Section 3.2 — Skills in transition]

We classify the 88 skills tracked in our taxonomy into four categories using a consensus-weighted index of WEF, McKinsey, CEPAL, and Frey-Osborne (2013) projections:

- "Retire": skill demand projected to fall by ≥50% by 2030. Includes manual journal entries, basic tax compliance reporting, contract template drafting, jurisprudence database lookup, and standard medical record transcription.
- "Redesign": skill demand stable but content changes substantially. Includes financial analysis (now requires AI tool literacy), legal research (now requires prompt engineering), and clinical diagnosis (now requires AI-output validation).
- "Reinforce": skill demand growing >25% by 2030. Includes algorithmic auditing, ESG reporting, behavioral therapy, and prompt-engineered programming.
- "Add": skills not currently in any program but projected to be required. Includes AI safety officer, model-risk auditor, computational ethics, and cross-functional AI translator.

The "Add" category represents the largest curricular gap: 71% of programs surveyed have no exposure to any of these skills.

[Section 3.3 — Recommendations for higher education leadership]

We recommend three immediate actions for university rectors and academic directors:

1. Conduct a curricular skills audit using a documented, reproducible methodology (e.g. the OIA-EE benchmark framework, used in 17 Mexican careers as a reference example).

2. Prioritize "Retire" and "Add" categories first. The "Redesign" category requires more nuance and is best handled in coordination with employers.

3. Treat curricular updates as continuous rather than periodic. The 2.1-year half-life of technical curricula means triennial reviews are no longer sufficient; quarterly reviews aligned with employer feedback loops are required.

[Conclusion]

The 2026 evidence confirms that generative AI is not eliminating professions in Latin America at scale, but it is rapidly transforming the skill composition of nearly every white-collar role. The university programs that respond by Q3 2027 will graduate cohorts that are competitive in a transformed market; those that do not will increasingly produce graduates whose first-job functions are already automated.
